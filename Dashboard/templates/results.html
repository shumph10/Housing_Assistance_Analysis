Preprocessing

Our final dataset is comprised of multiple data sets, all coming from the US Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). This reliable government source provides the most recent data for the past year, allowing comparison of Section 8 housing after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. It additionally is an accurate & credible source for the information the organization itself uses to make decisions on behalf of housing voucher assistance programs. Datasets from this source needed further processing to meet the needs of the project, including joining on common columns, removal of null and duplicates, and encoding, all of which are discussed in further detail in the sections beside.

Data Selection

Data sets were selected first to meet the needs of the project.
These included information on the key features of decision making for housing voucher amount which included fair market rates (FMR) and wait times for placement of new applicants.
Additional features of interest included location data for county and state, metro/non, percent and amount of utilities paid, income limits for applicants, and household income.
Since we are analyzing the difference pre-/post-pandemic, data was gathered for both 2017 and 2022.

Combination of Datasets

Once data was gathered, it was joined to supply one final dataset that could be used for analysis.
First, the Section 8 FMR datasets for 2017 & 2022 were joined through a full inner join to keep all information pertaining to FMRâ€™s, income limits, and metro/non encoding.
Then, the county datasets for 2017 & 2022 were joined, which supplied information on wait, utilities, household income.
Both datasets contained location information such as state and county code.
The state and county codes were formatted and concatenated in order to join the Section 8 and county datasets on a common column.
These were used to join the two resulting datasets into our final dataset for analysis.
Further processing of this data set will be discussed in the section to the right.

As datasets were being combined and after completion a series of steps were taken to ensure data remained accurate and intact.
First the data was checked for null and duplicate values. Since there was no other credible dataset to supplement this loss, and there were many null values that were not a valuable addition our analysis, null values were dropped.
The shape of the resulting dataframes were printed to analyze any loss of data, the resulting data frame had a reduction in size we decided collaborators deemed justified.
Data types of columns were adjusted as need to match information contained. The 2017 features were deducted from 2022 in instances where the differences between the years were directly studied.
Columns were renamed to represent their respective functions, rather that be indicative of year or difference.
Finally, a function was created to encode the state codes by official census region.

T-tests

Our project focuses on affordable housing voucher's strain on governmental assistance programs as rent prices for many across the US skyrocket. We will use data from the US Department of Housing and Urban Development(HUD) to establish the fair market price for 2 bedroom houses in 2017 and 2022 as this is the figure primarily used to establish amount of governmental assistance awarded, subtracting income based recipient payments. HUD describes fair housing prices idealy being below 30% of total household income, requireing that participants in either Section 8(general housing assistance) or Section 202(elderly housing assistance) pay a minimum 30% adjusted monthly income towards rent. The rest of rent is the responsibility of the HUD voucher program. Low income thresholds (those eligible for assistance) will be established, corresponding by county to determine the typical amount of rent paid by assistance recepients in that area for both years.
